# Data Quality And Incident Communication

Use this playbook when data may be incorrect, incomplete, delayed, inconsistent, or unsuitable for an intended use.
Use it for both internal coordination and client-facing updates, with confidentiality and technical depth adjusted to the audience.

## First Update

Communicate in this order:

1. What is known.
2. What is not yet known.
3. Affected data, dates, populations, products, or clients.
4. Current or potential impact.
5. Immediate containment or workaround.
6. Investigation owner.
7. Time of the next update.

Do not wait for a root cause before acknowledging a confirmed material issue.
Do not claim a root cause merely because one explanation is plausible.

## Quantify Scope

Prefer counts, percentages, date ranges, identifiers, and affected fields over adjectives such as "limited" or "small."
State the denominator and measurement time.

If scope is still changing, say that the figure is preliminary and explain what remains to be measured.
Do not provide a precise number that the current investigation cannot support.

## Impact

Separate:

- data correctness;
- data completeness;
- delivery timeliness;
- downstream analytical impact;
- client-visible impact;
- regulatory, contractual, or governance implications.

Do not assume that a data defect affected a client's result without tracing the downstream use.
Do not imply that absence of a reported client issue proves absence of impact.

## Ongoing Updates

Each update should state what changed since the previous update.
Repeat stable facts only when the message needs to stand alone.

Include:

- revised scope;
- confirmed or rejected hypotheses;
- containment status;
- remediation progress;
- validation performed;
- remaining risk;
- next update time.

## Resolution

A resolution message should cover:

- confirmed root cause;
- final measured scope and impact;
- correction or remediation;
- validation of the corrected data;
- client action, if any;
- prevention and monitoring changes;
- remaining limitations or follow-up work.

Separate root-cause explanation from ownership or blame.
Focus on the system and controls unless personnel detail is genuinely relevant and appropriate.

## Tone

Be calm, accountable, direct, and precise.
Signal urgency through impact, actions, and timestamps rather than dramatic language.
Avoid defensive phrasing, premature reassurance, and commitments that have not been confirmed.
