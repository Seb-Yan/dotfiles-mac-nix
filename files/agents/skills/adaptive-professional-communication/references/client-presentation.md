# Formal Client Presentations And Meetings

Use this playbook for client decks, formal meeting narratives, speaker notes, demonstrations of methodology, and Q&A preparation.

## Start With The Meeting Outcome

Define what the client should understand, believe, decide, or do by the end of the meeting.
Build the narrative around that outcome rather than around the order in which the analysis was performed.

Identify:

- the audience and their technical depth;
- the client's question or concern;
- the main conclusion;
- the strongest supporting evidence;
- the material limitation;
- the decision or next action.

## Narrative Structure

Use this default sequence:

1. Client objective or business question.
2. Main conclusion and why it matters.
3. Data coverage and methodology.
4. Validation and evidence.
5. Material limitations and uncertainty.
6. Implications for the client's use case.
7. Recommendation and next steps.

Reorder sections when the meeting purpose requires it.
For a data issue, establish known impact and remediation before presenting methodological background.

## Slide Design Through Language

Write slide titles as conclusions, not topic labels.

Prefer:

- "SOFR coverage supports an initial validation population."
- "Missing benchmark data limits direct spread reconstruction."
- "Out-of-sample errors remain concentrated in two loan segments."

Avoid:

- "SOFR analysis."
- "Methodology."
- "Results."

Each slide should make one main claim.
Use the minimum evidence needed to support that claim.
Move secondary methodology and definitions to an appendix when they are necessary for scrutiny but not for the main narrative.

## Speaker Notes

Speaker notes should add interpretation, transitions, caveats, and emphasis.
They should not repeat slide text word for word.

For each important slide, prepare:

- the one-sentence takeaway;
- the evidence to emphasize;
- the limitation to disclose;
- the transition to the next point;
- the likely client question.

## Q&A Preparation

Prepare questions in these categories:

- methodology and assumptions;
- data provenance and coverage;
- validation and error rates;
- financial interpretation;
- limitations and failure cases;
- implementation and operational use;
- delivery timing and roadmap;
- commercial or governance implications.

For each answer, distinguish verified facts from current assessment.
Identify questions that require follow-up rather than speculation.
Prepare a concise bridge such as: "We have not validated that segment yet, so I do not want to overstate the result. We can confirm the coverage and follow up with the measured impact."

## Delivery Tone

Be calm, precise, and confident about verified work.
Slow down for numbers, qualifications, and decisions.
Use technical vocabulary when it improves precision, then connect it to the client's use case.
Do not apologize for necessary limitations, but do not minimize them.

## Final Preparation

Verify that every numerical claim has a source and as-of date.
Check that terminology is consistent across slides, notes, and Q&A.
Confirm that recommendations follow from the evidence presented.
Prepare a short opening, a short closing, and a clear handoff for discussion.
