# Mentor And Manager Communication

Use this playbook for intern development, feedback, delegation, expectation setting, project direction, and performance conversations.

## Choose The Relationship Mode

Use mentor mode when the primary objective is to develop judgment and capability.
Use manager mode when the primary objective is alignment, delivery, accountability, or prioritization.
Combine them deliberately when an intern needs both a learning opportunity and a clear deliverable.

## Mentor Mode

Do not solve the entire problem before diagnosing how the person is thinking.

Use this progression:

1. Recognize what they understood, attempted, or improved.
2. Ask for or infer their reasoning and assumptions.
3. Identify the most important gap.
4. Explain the relevant research or engineering standard and why it matters.
5. Give a bounded next investigation without removing ownership.
6. Define the evidence that would constitute a satisfactory answer.
7. Set a checkpoint and expected artifact.

Adjust scaffolding to experience level.
A new intern may need examples and intermediate checkpoints.
A stronger intern should receive more room to define the method and defend tradeoffs.

Praise reasoning, evidence, learning, and ownership rather than innate ability.
Correct consequential errors directly, but make the standard teachable.

## Feedback

Prioritize feedback into:

- issues that invalidate the conclusion;
- issues that materially weaken confidence;
- improvements to clarity, efficiency, or presentation;
- optional extensions.

Do not overwhelm an intern with an undifferentiated list.
Separate feedback on the work from judgments about the person.

Useful feedback explains:

- what was observed;
- why it matters;
- what standard applies;
- what a stronger approach would demonstrate;
- what should happen next.

## Manager Mode

A substantial assignment should make these elements clear:

- context and why the work matters;
- expected outcome or decision;
- scope and explicit exclusions;
- known constraints and dependencies;
- definition of done;
- owner and checkpoint;
- decisions the owner can make independently;
- conditions that require escalation.

Do not prescribe implementation details that are not actually constraints.
Leave room for the owner to exercise judgment.

## Check-Ins

Use check-ins to surface decisions, evidence, risks, and blockers.
Do not turn them into activity recitations.

Ask:

- What changed in our understanding?
- What evidence supports the current direction?
- What is the largest unresolved risk?
- What decision is needed from me?
- What will be complete by the next checkpoint?

## Difficult Conversations

Be specific, timely, and respectful.
State the observed behavior or result, its impact, the expected standard, and the required change.
Avoid vague signals that force the recipient to guess whether there is a serious concern.
Allow room for relevant context without negotiating away the standard.
